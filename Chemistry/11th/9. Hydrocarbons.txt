Here are notes on the introduction, 9.1, and 9.2 topics for NEET, including key terms, explanations, and formulas from NCERT:

Introduction to Hydrocarbons

- Hydrocarbons: Compounds containing only carbon and hydrogen
- Main sources: Coal and petroleum
- Petrochemicals: Starting materials for many commercial products
- LPG (Liquefied Petroleum Gas) and CNG (Compressed Natural Gas): Major energy sources for domestic fuel and automobiles

9.1 Classification of Hydrocarbons

1. Open Chain (Aliphatic)
   a. Saturated: Alkanes (C-C single bonds only)
   b. Unsaturated: 
      - Alkenes (at least one C=C double bond)
      - Alkynes (at least one C≡C triple bond)

2. Closed Chain (Cyclic)
   a. Alicyclic: Saturated or unsaturated cyclic hydrocarbons
   b. Aromatic: Contain benzene rings or similar structures

9.2 Alkanes

- General formula: CnH2n+2
- Saturated hydrocarbons with single bonds between carbon atoms

9.2.1 Nomenclature and Isomerism

1. IUPAC Nomenclature:
   - Prefix based on number of carbon atoms (e.g., meth-, eth-, prop-, but-)
   - Suffix: -ane

2. Isomerism:
   - Structural isomerism: Same molecular formula, different structural arrangement
   - Types: Chain isomerism, position isomerism

9.2.2 Preparation of Alkanes

1. From unsaturated hydrocarbons:
   Hydrogenation: Addition of H2 gas in presence of catalysts (Pt, Pd, or Ni)
   Example: CH2=CH2 + H2 → CH3-CH3

2. From alkyl halides:
   a. Reduction with Zn and HCl:
      Example: CH3-Cl + H2 → CH4 + HCl

   b. Wurtz reaction: Using sodium in dry ether
      Example: CH3Br + 2Na + BrCH3 → CH3-CH3 + 2NaBr

3. From carboxylic acids:
   a. Decarboxylation of sodium salts with soda lime:
      Example: CH3COO-Na+ + NaOH → CH4 + Na2CO3

   b. Kolbe's electrolytic method:
      Example: 2CH3COO-Na+ + 2H2O → CH3-CH3 + 2CO2 + H2 + 2NaOH

9.2.3 Properties of Alkanes

Physical Properties:
- First four members (C1 to C4): Gases
- C5 to C17: Liquids
- C18 and above: Solids at room temperature
- Non-polar, insoluble in water
- Soluble in non-polar organic solvents

Chemical Properties:

1. Substitution reactions:
   Example: CH4 + Cl2 → CH3Cl + HCl (chlorination)

2. Combustion:
   Example: CH4 + 2O2 → CO2 + 2H2O

3. Controlled oxidation:
   Example: 2CH4 + O2 → 2CH3OH (methanol formation)

4. Isomerization:
   Example: n-Butane → Isobutane (in presence of AlCl3/HCl)

5. Aromatization:
   Example: n-Hexane → Benzene (at 773K and 10-20 atm pressure)

6. Reaction with steam:
   Example: CH4 + H2O → CO + 3H2 (at 1273K with Ni catalyst)

7. Pyrolysis:
   Thermal decomposition in absence of air

9.2.4 Conformations of Alkanes

- Conformations: Different spatial arrangements of atoms due to rotation around C-C single bonds
- Example: Ethane conformations
  a. Staggered conformation: More stable (hydrogen atoms furthest apart)
  b. Eclipsed conformation: Less stable (hydrogen atoms closer together)

Key Terms:
- Hydrogenation: Addition of hydrogen to a molecule
- Decarboxylation: Removal of CO2 from a carboxylic acid
- Electrolytic method: Using electricity to drive a chemical reaction
- Pyrolysis: Decomposition of a substance by heat in the absence of oxygen
- Conformation: Spatial arrangement of atoms in a molecule that can be interconverted by rotation around single bonds

Here are notes on topics 9.3 and 9.4 for NEET, including key terms, explanations, and formulas from NCERT:

9.3 Alkenes

- General formula: CnH2n
- Unsaturated hydrocarbons with at least one carbon-carbon double bond

9.3.1 Structure of Double Bond

- C=C bond consists of one σ (sigma) bond and one π (pi) bond
- σ bond: Formed by head-on overlapping of sp² hybridized orbitals
- π bond: Formed by lateral overlapping of unhybridized p orbitals
- C=C bond length: 134 pm (shorter than C-C single bond: 154 pm)

9.3.2 Nomenclature

- IUPAC system: Replace -ane suffix of alkane with -ene
- Numbering starts from the end closer to the double bond
- Examples:
  CH3-CH=CH2: Propene
  CH3-CH2-CH=CH2: But-1-ene
  CH2=CH-CH=CH2: Buta-1,3-diene

9.3.3 Isomerism

1. Structural isomerism:
   - Chain isomerism
   - Position isomerism

2. Geometrical isomerism:
   - cis-trans isomerism due to restricted rotation around C=C bond
   - Example: cis-but-2-ene and trans-but-2-ene

9.3.4 Preparation of Alkenes

1. Dehydrohalogenation of alkyl halides:
   Example: CH3-CH2-Br + KOH (alc) → CH2=CH2 + KBr + H2O

2. Dehalogenation of vicinal dihalides:
   Example: CH2Br-CH2Br + Zn → CH2=CH2 + ZnBr2

3. Dehydration of alcohols:
   Example: CH3-CH2-OH → CH2=CH2 + H2O (in presence of conc. H2SO4)

9.3.5 Properties of Alkenes

Physical Properties:
- First three members: Gases
- Next fourteen: Liquids
- Higher alkenes: Solids

Chemical Properties:

1. Addition reactions:
   a. Hydrogenation: CH2=CH2 + H2 → CH3-CH3
   b. Halogenation: CH2=CH2 + Br2 → CH2Br-CH2Br
   c. Hydrohalogenation: CH3-CH=CH2 + HBr → CH3-CHBr-CH3 (Markovnikov's rule)
   d. Hydration: CH2=CH2 + H2O → CH3-CH2-OH

2. Oxidation:
   a. With cold KMnO4 (Baeyer's reagent): Forms vicinal diols
   b. With acidic KMnO4: Forms ketones and/or acids

3. Ozonolysis:
   Example: CH3-CH=CH-CH3 + O3 → CH3-CHO + CH3-CHO

4. Polymerization:
   Example: n(CH2=CH2) → (-CH2-CH2-)n (polyethene)

9.4 Alkynes

- General formula: CnH2n-2
- Unsaturated hydrocarbons with at least one carbon-carbon triple bond

9.4.1 Nomenclature and Isomerism

- IUPAC system: Replace -ane suffix of alkane with -yne
- Numbering starts from the end closer to the triple bond
- Examples:
  HC≡CH: Ethyne
  CH3-C≡CH: Propyne
- Exhibit chain isomerism and position isomerism

9.4.2 Structure of Triple Bond

- C≡C bond consists of one σ bond and two π bonds
- sp hybridized carbon atoms
- C≡C bond length: 120 pm (shorter than C=C and C-C bonds)

9.4.3 Preparation of Alkynes

1. From calcium carbide:
   CaC2 + 2H2O → Ca(OH)2 + C2H2

2. From vicinal dihalides:
   Example: CH2Br-CHBr-CH3 + 2KOH (alc) → CH≡C-CH3 + 2KBr + 2H2O

9.4.4 Properties of Alkynes

Physical Properties:
- First three members: Gases
- Next eight: Liquids
- Higher alkynes: Solids

Chemical Properties:

1. Acidic nature:
   HC≡CH + Na → HC≡C-Na+ + ½H2

2. Addition reactions:
   a. Hydrogenation: HC≡CH + 2H2 → CH3-CH3
   b. Halogenation: HC≡CH + 2Br2 → CHBr2-CHBr2
   c. Hydrohalogenation: HC≡CH + HCl → CH2=CHCl

3. Hydration:
   HC≡CH + H2O → CH3-CHO (in presence of H2SO4 and HgSO4)

4. Polymerization:
   n(HC≡CH) → (-CH=CH-)n (polyacetylene)

Key Terms:
- sp² hybridization: Mixing of one s and two p orbitals to form three hybrid orbitals
- sp hybridization: Mixing of one s and one p orbital to form two hybrid orbitals
- Markovnikov's rule: In hydrohalogenation, the halogen attaches to the carbon with fewer hydrogen atoms
- Ozonolysis: Cleavage of alkenes or alkynes using ozone
- Vicinal dihalides: Two halogen atoms on adjacent carbon atoms
- Dehydrohalogenation: Removal of a hydrogen and a halogen from adjacent carbon atoms

Here are notes on topics 9.5 and 9.6 for NEET, including key terms, explanations, and formulas from NCERT:

9.5 Aromatic Hydrocarbons

- Aromatic compounds (arenes): Cyclic compounds with delocalized π electrons
- Simplest example: Benzene (C6H6)

9.5.1 Nomenclature and Isomerism

- IUPAC system: Use prefix "phenyl-" or suffix "-benzene"
- Ortho (o-): 1,2-disubstituted
- Meta (m-): 1,3-disubstituted
- Para (p-): 1,4-disubstituted
- Examples:
  CH3-C6H5: Methylbenzene (Toluene)
  C6H5-C6H5: Biphenyl

9.5.2 Structure of Benzene

- Kekulé structure: Cyclic arrangement of six carbon atoms with alternating single and double bonds
- Resonance hybrid: Two equivalent Kekulé structures
- All C-C bonds equal length (139 pm)
- Planar molecule with 120° bond angles

9.5.3 Aromaticity

Hückel's Rule: Aromatic compounds have (4n + 2) π electrons, where n is an integer

Conditions for aromaticity:
1. Planarity
2. Complete delocalization of π electrons
3. Presence of (4n + 2) π electrons

9.5.4 Preparation of Benzene

1. Cyclic polymerization of ethyne:
   3HC≡CH → C6H6

2. Decarboxylation of aromatic acids:
   C6H5COONa + NaOH → C6H6 + Na2CO3

3. Reduction of phenol:
   C6H5OH + Zn → C6H6 + ZnO

9.5.5 Properties of Aromatic Compounds

Physical Properties:
- Generally colorless liquids or solids
- Characteristic aroma
- Insoluble in water, soluble in organic solvents

Chemical Properties:

1. Electrophilic substitution reactions:

a. Halogenation:
   C6H6 + Cl2 → C6H5Cl + HCl (in presence of FeCl3)

b. Nitration:
   C6H6 + HNO3 + H2SO4 → C6H5NO2 + H2O

c. Sulphonation:
   C6H6 + H2SO4 → C6H5SO3H + H2O

d. Friedel-Crafts alkylation:
   C6H6 + CH3Cl → C6H5CH3 + HCl (in presence of anhydrous AlCl3)

e. Friedel-Crafts acylation:
   C6H6 + CH3COCl → C6H5COCH3 + HCl (in presence of anhydrous AlCl3)

2. Addition reactions (under special conditions):
   C6H6 + 3H2 → C6H12 (cyclohexane)

3. Combustion:
   C6H6 + 7½O2 → 6CO2 + 3H2O

9.5.6 Directive Influence of Functional Groups in Monosubstituted Benzene

1. Ortho and para directing groups:
   -NH2, -NHR, -OH, -OR, -R, -NHCOR
   Activate the ring, direct incoming groups to ortho and para positions

2. Meta directing groups:
   -NO2, -CN, -CHO, -COR, -COOH, -COOR
   Deactivate the ring, direct incoming groups to meta position

3. Halogens (-F, -Cl, -Br, -I):
   Deactivate the ring but direct to ortho and para positions

9.6 Carcinogenicity and Toxicity

- Benzene and polynuclear hydrocarbons (with more than two fused benzene rings) can be toxic and carcinogenic
- Formed by incomplete combustion of organic materials (e.g., tobacco, coal, petroleum)
- Can damage DNA and cause cancer

Examples of carcinogenic hydrocarbons:
1. Benzo[a]pyrene
2. 3,4-Benzpyrene
3. 1,2,5,6-Dibenzanthracene

Key Terms:
- Arenes: Aromatic hydrocarbons
- Resonance: The use of two or more Lewis structures to represent a particular molecule
- Delocalization: The distribution of electrons over several atoms in a molecule
- Electrophilic substitution: Reaction where an electrophile replaces an atom or group in a compound
- Carcinogenic: Cancer-causing
- Polynuclear hydrocarbons: Compounds containing multiple fused aromatic rings
